Introduction

Fabric is the foundation of every garment. It not only shapes the appearance and functionality of clothing, but also influences the mood, movement, and overall design of a piece. The choice of fabric can make or break a fashion project—it defines how a garment drapes, feels on the body, and even how it performs over time.

This guide offers a practical, technical overview of fabrics from a designer’s perspective. It explores core fabric types, fiber properties, construction techniques, finishes, and sustainability concerns, as well as guidance on fabric sourcing and documentation. Whether you're a student, a practicing designer, or just passionate about textiles, this resource is designed to deepen your understanding and improve your design process.

Basic Types of Fabrics

Fabrics are typically classified into three primary types based on the origin and processing of their fibers: natural, synthetic, and artificial (or semi-synthetic). This classification helps designers evaluate a fabric’s behavior, properties and sustainability profile.

Natural Fibers

These are made from raw fibers derived directly from plants or animals, with minimal chemical processing. Natural fabrics tend to be breathable, biodegradable, and comfortable against the skin. They respond well to dyeing and finishing techniques but may wrinkle easily or shrink without proper treatment. Common examples include cotton, linen, hemp, and ramie (plant-based), as well as silk, wool, cashmere, and mohair (animal-based).

Synthetic Fibers

Fully man-made through polymerization of petrochemical compounds, synthetic fabrics are engineered for durability, elasticity, and resistance to moisture and abrasion. They often have low breathability but are lightweight, fast-drying, and cost-effective to produce at scale. Common examples are polyester, polyamide (nylon), acrylic, and elastane.

Semi-synthetic Fibers

Unlike synthetic fibers made from petrochemicals, semi-synthetics retain a natural base structure. These fabrics are made from natural raw materials like wood pulp, which contain long-chain polymers (usually cellulose). The fibers are chemically processed and regenerated, but not fully synthetic. The most common examples are viscose, modal, lyocell, and cupro.

Fibers and Their Properties

Natural Fibers

Cotton

Cotton is a natural fiber harvested from the cotton plant, with a cultivation history that spans thousands of years. It produces fabric that is lightweight, soft, and highly breathable. Thanks to its excellent moisture absorption, cotton fabrics are very comfortable to wear in warm weather. This makes cotton a healthy choice for everyday clothing, especially for those with sensitive skin or allergies.

Common uses: T-shirts, dresses, shirts, bedsheets, casual wear, denim.

Linen

Linen is a natural fiber obtained from the flax plant. It is valued for its exceptional strength, breathability, and moisture-wicking abilities. Lightweight and cool to the touch, linen provides excellent comfort in warm weather.While linen wrinkles more easily than many other fabrics, this texture is often appreciated as part of its natural character. To reduce creasing and stiffness, linen is frequently blended with other fibers, improving its softness but comforting durability.

Common uses: Summer clothing, suits, dresses, tablecloths, upholstery.

Wool

Wool is a natural animal fiber obtained primarily from sheep. It has been used in textiles for thousands of years, especially in colder climates due to its excellent insulation, durability and moisture-wicking properties. Wool fibers naturally repel moisture while allowing breathability, keeping the wearer warm and dry. They resist wrinkling and maintain their shape well, thanks to their natural elasticity. However, wool requires careful handling to avoid shrinking or felting during washing.

There are several specialty wools, each with unique origins and characteristics:

  • Wool: from sheep, offering a balance of warmth, resilience, and breathability, though it can sometimes irritate the skin and cause allergies.
  • Cashmere: sourced from the undercoat of Kashmir goats, known for its exceptional softness and lightweight warmth.
  • Mohair: derived from the Angora goat, mohair fibers are strong, silky, and have a natural sheen with good elasticity.
  • Alpaca: harvested from alpacas native to South America, this fiber is soft, lightweight, and warmer than sheep’s wool, with excellent hypoallergenic properties.
  • Angora: comes from Angora rabbits, producing a fine, fluffy fiber renowned for its exceptional softness and insulation.

Common uses: Coats, sweaters, scarves, suits, blankets, and upholstery.

Silk

Silk is a natural protein fiber produced by silkworms, valued for its luxurious softness, smooth texture, and natural sheen. With a history spanning over 4,000 years, silk has long been a symbol of elegance and high-quality textiles.

Silk fabric is lightweight, breathable, and drapes beautifully, making it ideal for garments requiring fluidity and refinement. It offers moderate elasticity and is naturally hypoallergenic, gentle on sensitive skin. However, silk is delicate and requires careful care as it can weaken with prolonged exposure to moisture or sunlight.

Common uses: Evening gowns, scarves, lingerie, ties, luxury bedding.

Synthetic Fibers

Polyester

Polyester is a fully synthetic fiber made from petrochemicals, primarily derived from petroleum. It is strong, durable, wrinkle-resistant, and holds its shape well. Polyester dries quickly and is often blended with natural fibers to improve performance and reduce cost.

While it's affordable and versatile, polyester is not breathable and can trap moisture and odors, which can lead to skin irritations and even infections. It’s also non-biodegradable and sheds microplastics during washing, which contributes to water pollution.

Common uses: Sportswear, outerwear, everyday clothing, home textiles like curtains and upholstery.

Polyamide

Polyamide, commonly known as nylon, is a strong, lightweight synthetic fiber known for its excellent abrasion resistance and elasticity. It dries quickly and has good wrinkle resistance, making it ideal for activewear and durable textiles. While nylon is durable and versatile, it is non-breathable and can retain odors, just like polyester. It's just as well non-biodegradable and contributes to microplastic pollution during washing.

Common uses: Activewear, hosiery, swimwear, jackets and outdoor, luggage, upholstery.

Elastane

Elastane, known also as spandex or Lycra®, is a highly elastic synthetic fiber made from polyurethane. Known for its exceptional stretch and recovery, it can expand up to five times its length and return to its original shape. It’s rarely used alone but blended with other fibers to add flexibility and comfort. Elastane is lightweight and smooth, but it’s non-biodegradable and sensitive to heat and harsh chemicals, so elastane blends need careful handling.

Common uses: Sportswear, swimwear, leggings, underwear, fitted garments.

Acrylic

Acrylic is designed to mimic the softness and warmth of wool. It is lightweight, quick-drying, and resistant to moths, sunlight, and chemicals. However, it tends to pill over time and it's not as breathable nor durable as wool. Acrylic holds color well and is often used as a low-cost alternative to natural fibers, but it's important to notice it's non-biodegradable, contributing to microplastic pollution. It's also flammable and should be handled with care near heat.

Common uses: Knitwear, sweaters, scarves, blankets, faux fur, and home textiles.

Semi-Synthetic Fibers

Viscose

Viscose is made by chemically processing cellulose from wood pulp. It combines the breathability of natural fibers with the smooth, silky feel of synthetic fabrics. Viscose drapes well, is lightweight, and absorbs moisture effectively, making it comfortable to wear. However, viscose wrinkles easily and can weaken and stretch when wet, so it requires gentle care.

Common uses: Dresses, skirts, blouses, scarves, linings.

Cupro

Cupro is a regenerated cellulose fiber made from cotton linter, the short fibers left after cotton processing. It has a smooth, silky texture and excellent breathability. Cupro drapes beautifully, feels cool on the skin, and is highly absorbent, which makes it very similar to silk. Cupro as well requires delicate care to prevent shrinking and wrinkles.

Common uses: Blouses, dresses, shirts, scarves.

Lyocell

Lyocell, often known by the brand name Tencel, is a regenerated cellulose fiber made from sustainably sourced wood pulp, processed using a closed-loop system that recycles nearly all chemicals and water. It’s soft, breathable, and has excellent moisture-wicking properties, making it comfortable and cool to wear. It’s commonly blended with other fibers to improve fabric performance.

Common uses: Casual wear, dresses, trousers, activewwear.

Modal

Modal is a type of regenerated cellulose fiber made from beech tree pulp. It is soft, smooth, and breathable, with excellent moisture absorption, making it comfortable for everyday wear. Modal drapes well and resists shrinking and pilling better than regular rayon. It's often used in knitted fabrics for enhanced activewear.

Common uses: Underwear, activewear, loungewear, dresses.

You may also come across the name Rayon. Rayon is the umbrella term for a group of regenerated cellulose fibers made from plant-based sources like wood pulp. Though it includes several types, such as viscose, modal, and lyocell, it usually refers to viscose, which is the most common and traditional form of rayon.

Weaves and Fabric Structures

Fabrics are typically constructed using two main techniques: weaving and knitting.

Woven fabrics are made by interlacing two sets of yarns—warp (vertical) and weft (horizontal)—on a loom. This creates a stable, structured fabric with limited stretch.

Knitted fabrics are formed by interlooping yarns into a series of rows (courses) and columns (wales). This looped structure makes them naturally stretchy, soft, and flexible.

This section focuses on woven fabrics, where the way threads cross determines the weave pattern, influencing the fabric’s texture, drape, strength, and visual appearance. Different types of weaves offer varying characteristics suited to different design and functional needs. Here are some basic types of weaves.

Plain Weave

Plain weave is the most basic and widely used weaving structure. It’s created by alternating the warp and weft threads over and under each other in a consistent one-to-one pattern. This results in a flat, balanced surface with equal tension in both directions. Plain weave fabrics tend to be firm, durable, and resistant to snagging. While they don’t drape as fluidly as some other weaves, they are easy to sew and versatile in use.

Examples: cotton poplin, muslin, voile, chiffon, organza.

Twill Weave

Twill weave is characterized by diagonal lines on the fabric surface, created by offsetting the interlacing of warp and weft threads. The most typical pattern is “over two, under one,” repeated in staggered rows. Twill fabrics are strong, flexible, and more resistant to wrinkles and creases than plain weaves. They also have a softer drape and are commonly used in workwear and tailored garments.

Examples: denim, chino, gabardine, houndstooth.

Satin Weave

Satin weave creates a smooth, glossy surface by floating the warp or weft threads over multiple yarns before interlacing. These long floats reflect light, giving the fabric its characteristic shine. The result is a fluid, elegant fabric with excellent drape but lower resistance to abrasion and snagging. Satin is often used where a luxurious look and feel are desired.

Examples: silk satin, charmeuse, duchess satin.

Herringbone Weave

Herringbone is a decorative variation of the twill weave. It forms a zigzag or “V-shaped” pattern by reversing the direction of the diagonal twill at regular intervals. This weave combines the strength and softness of twill with a sophisticated, textured appearance. It's especially popular in classic menswear and interior textiles.

Examples: wool suiting, outerwear fabrics, tweeds.

Basket Weave

Basket weave is a variation of plain weave in which two or more warp and weft threads are grouped together and woven in pairs (e.g., two-over-two). This produces a looser, more flexible structure with a subtle checkerboard texture. Basket weaves are breathable and often used in casual or sporty garments.

Examples: canvas, oxford.

Jacquard

Jacquard weave allows for the creation of intricate and highly detailed woven patterns, such as florals, brocades, or geometric designs. It’s made using a special loom that controls each warp thread individually. These fabrics are often heavier and more structured, with complex textures and visual depth. Because of their decorative nature, jacquard weaves are frequently used in formalwear, upholstery, and high-end fashion.

Examples: brocade, damask, matelassé, jacquard knits.

Fabric Finishes

Fabrics undergo various finishing processes after weaving or knitting to enhance their appearance, texture, durability, and functionality. These finishes can improve comfort, protect against elements, add decorative effects, or make care easier. Below are common types of finishes and treatments used in textile production.

Fabric Finishes That Affect Fabric Properties

Mercerization

A chemical treatment for cotton that increases luster, strength, and dye affinity by swelling and straightening fibers under tension. Results in a smoother, shinier fabric that resists shrinkage. Common in quality cotton garments and linens.

Brushing

Raising surface fibers with fine brushes to create a soft, fuzzy texture. Enhances warmth and comfort with a cozy matte look. Used in flannel, fleece, and winter-weight cotton.

Calendering

Fabric passes through heated rollers to smooth and polish the surface, adding sheen and improving drape. Common on synthetic blends, sateens, and linings.

Waterproofing / Water-Repellent Finishes

Protective coatings that resist or block water, either breathable (water-repellent) or fully waterproof. Used in rainwear, outdoor gear, and tents.

Anti-wrinkle / Easy-care Finish

Chemical treatments for natural fibers to reduce creasing and improve shape retention, minimizing ironing. Common in dress shirts, uniforms, and businesswear.

Flame Retardant Finish

Finishes that reduce flammability and slow fire spread, essential for protective clothing, children’s wear, upholstery, and curtains. Can be durable or temporary.

Specialty and Aesthetic Fabric Finishes

Stone Washing

Using pumice stones or abrasives to soften fabric and create a faded, worn look. Adds comfort and reduces stiffness, mostly used for denim and casual cotton.

Enzyme Washing

Eco-friendly and milder alternative to stone washing, using natural enzymes to break down fibers for softness and subtle fading. Common in cotton, linen, and viscose, to soften and add smooth handfeeling.

Sandblasting

Another finishing applicable to denim. It's a mechanical finish where high-pressure air mixed with abrasive particles is used to distress fabric surfaces. It gives textiles a rugged, vintage appearance. This technique is being replaced by safer, more sustainable methods due to health concerns in production.

Coating / Laminating

Applying materials like polyurethane, acrylic, rubber or wax to add gloss, structure, or functionality—like waterproofing or a leather-like effect. Coated fabrics are often less breathable but highly durable. Used in rainwear, fashion outerwear, and accessories.

Crinkling / Pleating

Mechanical or chemical processes are used to create permanent texture or folds in the fabric. This adds dimension and visual interest while affecting drape. Examples include crushed velvet, accordion pleats.

Dyeing

Dyeing is the process of adding color to textile fibers, yarns, or fabrics using various dye substances. It can be done at different stages—fiber, yarn, fabric, or garment—and each method affects colorfastness, texture, and depth of tone.

Natural fibers generally absorb dyes better than synthetics. The method of dyeing—such as vat dyeing, reactive dyeing, or pigment dyeing—also determines the durability and intensity of color.

Common dyeing styles include solid color, ombré (gradient), or tie-dye effects.

Printing

Printing involves applying color or design onto the surface of the fabric in specific patterns. It can be done using several techniques, including screen printing, digital printing, block printing, or rotary printing. Printed fabrics allow for highly detailed and multicolored designs, unlike dyeing which is usually uniform. The feel and durability of the print depend on the ink, fabric type, and printing method used. Another finishing, such as enzyme washing, can be added to soften the printed fabric.

Printing is used extensively in fashion, interiors and textile.

Sustainable Fabric Choices

As environmental awareness grows, the fashion and textile industries are shifting toward more responsible material choices. Sustainable fabrics aim to reduce harm to ecosystems, minimize water and energy use, and promote ethical labor practices. This includes both naturally low-impact fibers and innovative alternatives made from recycled or renewable sources.

Certifications play a key role in verifying whether materials meet environmental and ethical standards. Understanding these options helps designers make informed decisions that support both people and the planet.

Organic Fibers

Natural fibers are valued for their breathability, comfort, and ability to biodegrade. However, their environmental impact depends on how they’re cultivated and processed. While they decompose more easily than synthetics, treatments like chemical dyeing can still harm the environment.

Conventional cotton farming poses major sustainability issues due to high water use and heavy reliance on pesticides and fertilizers, which degrade soil and pollute waterways. In contrast, organic cotton is grown without synthetic chemicals, using less water and supporting soil health and biodiversity.

Flax cultivation, used to make organic linen typically requires fewer pesticides and less water than cotton, so it's naturally a more sustainable choice. Similarly, organic wool comes from sheep raised with strict animal welfare standards and sustainable land management.

Certifications such as GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard) and OEKO-TEX play a crucial role in promoting these sustainable practices. GOTS certifies organic fiber production from farming through processing, ensuring strict environmental and social criteria. OEKO-TEX certifies that textiles are free from harmful chemicals, providing further assurance of sustainability and safety.

TENCEL™ Lyocell / Modal

Produced using FSC-certified sustainably managed wood sources, these fibers are manufactured in closed-loop systems that recycle almost all chemicals and water. This process minimizes waste and pollution, making TENCEL™ fibers biodegradable, breathable, and soft, with excellent moisture management.

EcoVero™

A sustainable variant of viscose, Lenzing™ EcoVero™ is made by from certified renewable wood sources with a production process that reduces emissions and water consumption by up to 50% compared to traditional viscose. It offers similar comfort and drape but with a significantly lower environmental footprint.

Cupro

Derived from cotton waste (specifically cotton linters, the short fibers left after ginning), Cupro is a biodegradable, silky fabric often considered a more eco-friendly alternative to silk. Its production reuses agricultural by-products, reducing raw material demand.

Recycled Fibers

Recycled fibers transform post-consumer or post-industrial waste into new textile materials, conserving resources and diverting waste from landfills and oceans.

Recycled Polyester (rPET)

Made by melting down and re-spinning plastic bottles, recycled polyester reduces dependence on fossil fuels and cuts greenhouse gas emissions compared to virgin polyester. It retains durability and moisture resistance, widely used in activewear, outerwear, and accessories.

Recycled Nylon (e.g., ECONYL®)

Created by regenerating nylon waste such as discarded fishing nets and fabric scraps, recycled nylon offers the same strength and elasticity as new nylon but with a smaller carbon footprint. It is commonly used in swimwear, hosiery, and technical apparel.

Choosing the Right Fabric for a Design

Different garments require fabrics that support their intended shape, movement, and function. The right textile choice enhances comfort, durability, and fit, while poor fabric selection can result in garments that drape poorly, wear out quickly, or feel uncomfortable.

When choosing a fabric, consider:

  • Structure: Does the design require stiffness or fluidity?
  • Weight: Lighter fabrics work better for flowing silhouettes; heavier fabrics add shape and warmth.
  • Stretch: Knit or elastane blends are better for fitted garments.
  • Durability: Outerwear and tailored garments need strength and resilience.
  • Drape and handfeeling: Affects the way fabric falls and feels.

Technical Documentation

Providing accurate and detailed fabric information is essential in fashion production. The people who turn your design into a finished garment—pattern makers, seamstresses, suppliers, and production managers—rely on this data to correctly handle, cut, and sew the material. Incomplete or vague fabric documentation can lead to miscommunication, poor garment quality, or production delays.

Why Details Matter

Each fabric behaves differently. A stretch fabric requires a different pattern and sewing technique than a rigid woven. A loosely woven wool may need stabilizing, while a delicate silk demands gentle handling. By specifying how a fabric behaves, you help ensure the final product meets design expectations in terms of fit, drape, durability, and comfort.

Key Information to Include

  • Fabric Composition (%): Lists fiber content (e.g. 70% cotton / 30% polyester). Helps determine stretch, shrinkage, care instructions, and behavior during sewing.
  • Fabric Weight (g/m²): Indicates heaviness or thickness. Affects drape, structure, and seasonality of the garment.
  • Weave or Knit Type: Plain weave, twill, jersey, etc. Impacts drape, flexibility, and construction techniques.
  • Finishing: Any treatments like pre-shrinking, brushing, waterproofing, or special coatings. Important for how the fabric behaves in use and during production.

Optional but Useful:

  • Stretch % (if applicable): For knits or fabrics with elastane, helps with pattern adjustment.
  • Shrinkage % (if applicable): For fabrics undergoing heat or heavy treatments, like printing or stone-washing (denim)
  • Fabric Width (cm): Needed to calculate fabric consumption.
  • Care Instructions: Guides pre-washing and post-production care.
  • Supplier / Code / Origin: For traceability and re-ordering.

A well-documented fabric spec ensures consistency, quality, and smooth collaboration from design to production.

Where to Source Fabrics

Finding the right fabric is a key step in the design process. Whether you're sourcing for sampling or full production, knowing where and how to look is essential. Here's an overview of the most common fabric sourcing options and what to keep in mind.

Types of Suppliers:

Wholesale Suppliers

Ideal for bulk buying and production. They often offer competitive pricing but may require minimum order quantities. Expect access to professional swatches, fabric specs, and repeat availability.

Online stores

Convenient for sampling or small runs. Some online shops offer detailed descriptions, but quality and color may differ from photos—always request swatches when possible.

Boutique Fabric Shops

Great for unique finds and smaller quantities. These are ideal for prototypes or one-off garments but often more expensive and less predictable in availability.

Fabric Trade Shows and Sourcing Fairs

Events like Première Vision or local fabric expos offer direct contact with mills and suppliers. These fairs are perfect for discovering new materials, innovations, and building sourcing relationships.

Key Things to Check Before Buying Fabric:

  • Composition: fiber content tells you about basic fabric properties and care. For tight, fit designs identify if fabric contains elastane.
  • Fabric Weight (g/m² or oz/yd²): it will affect desired structure, drape, and seasonality.
  • Width: Most fabrics are 110–160 cm wide. Knowing this helps you calculate how much you’ll need.
  • Color Accuracy: Fabric photos online may vary from reality due to lighting or screen settings. Request swatches if possible.
  • Minimum Orders and Returns: Be aware of fabric cut policies (many don't allow returns) and whether fabrics are sold by meter, yard, or panel.
  • Shipping Costs: Be aware of shipping fees, especially for international orders. Keep in mind that fabrics can be heavy, especially in bulk.
Glossary of Terms
  • Breathability: A fabric’s ability to allow air and moisture vapor to pass through, affecting comfort.
  • Drape: How a fabric falls or hangs on a body or form. Influenced by weight, weave, and fiber type.
  • EcoVero™: A sustainable viscose brand made with reduced water and emissions.
  • Fabric: A material made by weaving, knitting, or bonding fibers together.
  • Fabric Composition: The percentages of different fibers that make up a fabric (e.g., 70% cotton, 30% polyester).
  • Fiber: The basic raw material used to make yarns and fabrics. Can be natural, synthetic, or semi-synthetic.
  • Finish: A chemical or mechanical treatment applied to fabric to change its appearance, texture, or performance (e.g., coating, brushing).
  • GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard): A leading certification for organic fibers and sustainable textile production.
  • GSM (Grams per Square Meter): A measure of fabric weight, indicating thickness and density.
  • Knit Fabric: Fabric made by looping yarns together, forming rows and columns. Known for stretch and comfort.
  • Moisture Wicking: The ability of a fabric to pull moisture away from the skin and help it evaporate.
  • OEKO-TEX®: A certification that ensures a fabric is free from harmful substances.
  • Recycled Polyester (rPET): Polyester fiber made from recycled plastic, such as PET bottles.
  • Regenerated Fiber: A type of semi-synthetic fiber made by chemically processing natural materials (e.g., viscose, modal).
  • Selvedge: The tightly woven edge of a fabric that prevents unraveling.
  • Swatch: A small sample of fabric used to evaluate texture, color, and quality before purchasing.
  • TENCEL™ / Lyocell: A branded semi-synthetic fiber made from wood pulp using a closed-loop, low-impact process.
  • Warp and Weft Warp threads run vertically and are held in tension on a loom; weft threads are woven horizontally through the warp.
  • Weave: The pattern in which threads (warp and weft) are interlaced to create a fabric (e.g., plain, twill, satin).
